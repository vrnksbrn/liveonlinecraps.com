How to Play Craps: A Beginner’s Guide to the Rules

The craps table, with its myriad of betting boxes, can seem incredibly intimidating to the uninitiated. However, the game’s fundamental mechanics are surprisingly straightforward. The digital interface when you play live dealer craps online is a massive help, as it highlights valid betting areas and guides you through each phase of the game. Let’s break it down.

The Game’s Two Phases: The Come-Out Roll and The Point

Every round of craps revolves around a two-stage process, which begins with the Come-Out Roll. This is the first roll of the dice in a new betting round, and the online interface will clearly indicate when it is happening.

  • The Roll’s Outcome: The result of this first roll determines what happens next.

    1. A Natural (7 or 11): If the dice total 7 or 11, the most common bet (the Pass Line bet) wins immediately. The round is over, and a new Come-Out roll begins.

    2. Craps (2, 3, or 12): If the dice total 2, 3, or 12, the Pass Line bet loses instantly. The round ends, and the game resets for a new Come-Out roll.

    3. The Point (4, 5, 6, 8, 9, or 10): If any of these numbers are rolled, that number becomes the “Point.” The dealer places a puck marked “ON” on that number on the table layout. The game now enters its second phase.

  • After the Point is Established: The objective of the game now changes. The dealer will continue to roll the dice. For players who bet on the Pass Line, the goal is for the dealer to roll the Point number again before rolling a 7. If the Point is rolled, they win. If a 7 is rolled (a “Seven-Out”), they lose, the round ends, and a new Come-Out roll begins.

The Foundational Wagers: Pass Line and Don’t Pass Line

For anyone just starting to play craps live online, these two bets are the only ones you need to know initially. They are the bedrock of the game and offer some of the best odds for the player in the entire casino.

  • The Pass Line Bet: This is the most common and social bet on the table. You are betting with the shooter (the dealer in the online version). You win on a Natural (7, 11) on the Come-Out roll and lose on Craps (2, 3, 12). If a Point is established, you win if that Point is rolled again before a 7. It’s a simple wager with a very low house edge of just 1.41%.

  • The Don’t Pass Line Bet: This is the opposite wager, where you bet against the shooter. You win if the Come-Out roll is a 2 or 3 (a 12 is a “push,” meaning your bet is returned) and lose on a 7 or 11. If a Point is established, you are now betting that a 7 will be rolled before the Point number. While it can feel like you’re betting against the table, it offers a slightly more favorable house edge of 1.36%.

Diving Deeper: Understanding the Full Range of Craps Bets

Once you are comfortable with the basic flow of the game, the rest of the table’s betting options open up a world of strategic possibilities. The digital interface makes placing these more complex wagers incredibly simple, allowing you to expand your horizons with just a few clicks.

Multi-Roll Bets: Building Your Presence on the Table

These bets allow you to have action on multiple numbers at once, making the game far more dynamic after the main Point has been set.

  • The Come Bet: This bet functions exactly like a personal Pass Line bet, but you can place it at any time after the main Point is established. The very next roll of the dice serves as the “come-out roll” for your Come bet. A 7 or 11 is an instant win; a 2, 3, or 12 is an instant loss. If any other number is rolled, that becomes your personal “Come Point,” and your chip is moved to that number. You then win if your Come Point is rolled before a 7.

  • The Don’t Come Bet: As you can guess, this is the inverse of the Come bet and works like a personal Don’t Pass bet. You place it after the main point is on, and you win on a 2 or 3, push on a 12, and lose on a 7 or 11 on the next roll. If a point is established, you win if a 7 comes before that number.

  • Place Bets: This is a more straightforward way to bet on specific numbers. You can place a bet directly on the 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, or 10. By placing the number, you are simply wagering that it will be rolled before a 7 appears. These bets are “working” on every roll unless you ask the dealer to turn them off. They pay out at slightly less than true odds, with the 6 and 8 offering the best value.

Proposition Bets: High-Risk, High-Reward Wagers

The center section of the craps table is reserved for “Proposition Bets.” These are typically high-payout, one-roll bets that come with a significantly higher house edge. They should be viewed as fun, lottery-style wagers to spice up the game, not as a core part of a sound strategy. Examples include the “Field Bet” (a bet on the next roll being a 2, 3, 4, 9, 10, 11, or 12) and “Any 7” (a bet that the next roll will be exactly 7). While the payouts can be tempting, their high house edge makes them a losing proposition over the long term.
